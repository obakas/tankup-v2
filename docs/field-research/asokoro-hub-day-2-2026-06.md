# Asokoro Tanker Hub — Field Visit, Day 2 (June 2026)

## Questions prepared

Deeper operational questions, following on from Day 1:

1. What causes the longest delays in a day?
2. How do customers usually complain?
3. Which areas are hardest to deliver to?
4. What makes a driver reject a job?
5. How do they handle fake promises/nonpayment?
6. What time of day gets most requests?
7. Do they lose customers because they miss calls?
8. Do they have trusted repeat customers?
9. What happens when tankers break down mid-delivery?
10. How do they coordinate during high demand season?
11. Do they already have informal dispatch systems?

## What happened / answers

The hub leader from Day 1 wasn't around; the deputy answered the 11 questions instead.

1. **Longest delays:** No real delay in the delivery process itself — if anything, the delay is in negotiating payment.
2. **Customer complaints:** Some customers have oversized or undersized tanks, and complain the water delivered isn't enough for an oversized tank. He reiterated that what the customer is really paying for is the diesel, not the water itself.
3. **Hardest areas:** Lugbe, Airport Road, Orozo — there's no water lifting point near those areas.
4. **Why drivers reject jobs:** When the customer's tank position is very high, or the distance from where the tanker parks to the customer's tank is very far. Tankers usually don't know this in advance — but experienced drivers learn to reject a given customer on their next request. Some tankers even turn back mid-job, accepting it as a loss, to avoid damaging their pumping engine.
5. **Nonpayment / bad-faith customers:** Some customers — generals, military, other high-position individuals — bully or shout at drivers, refusing to pay for water already delivered. Some underpay, promising to pay the remaining balance later and then don't. Some refuse to pay until the tanker completes impossible extra tasks (e.g. filling all their containers at inaccessible spots). Others pay in full with no issue.
6. **Peak request times:** Early morning and late evening, but requests can come in at any time, 24/7.
7. **Lost customers from missed calls:** Yes.
8. **Trusted repeat customers:** Yes, they have them.
9. **Mid-delivery breakdowns:** If urgent, they transfer the water load to another tanker, or send a replacement tanker.
10. **Coordinating high demand:** They give the customer an expected delivery time. Some customers don't want to wait and will pay more to fast-track their delivery during high-demand periods.
11. **Informal dispatch systems:** Yes — both formal and informal. Formal clients include banks, the presidential villa, barracks, and hotels.

## Other notes from the conversation

- After the Q&A, the app idea ("like Uber, for water") was pitched directly. The deputy understood quickly and reacted positively.
- He repeatedly emphasized that the hardest operational problem is identifying customers with very high tanks before arrival — it strains the pumping machine, and he was skeptical that technology could solve that at all.
- Reiterated: customers are paying for diesel, not water — water itself is nearly free; the cost is in delivering and pumping it.
- After a successful delivery, any remaining water is thrown away to lighten the vehicle and save diesel on the return trip.
- Pitched the flow meter idea and the batch delivery idea — he wasn't fully sold on either, but it wasn't pushed further.
- No drinks/minerals were brought this time (as instructed) — they didn't ask for any.
